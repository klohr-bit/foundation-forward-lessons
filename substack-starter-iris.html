Your First Substack Post

A guided walk-through

Hi Iris. Let's build your first Substack post.

By the end of this, you'll have an actual draft. Not a plan to write one. A real first post, built from work you've already done, ready to paste into Substack and hit publish. It should take about fifteen minutes. Go slow. Your work saves as you go, so you can close the tab and come back later if you need to.

Before we start
This is not a lecture. It's a workbook. I'll explain things as we go, and I'll ask you to paste your own work into a few boxes. Think of this as a first draft space, not a final anything. Perfect is the enemy here.

Step 01 of 04 · What it is

What Substack actually is, and whether it's for you.

Substack is an email newsletter that also lives on the web as your own little site. When you publish a post, it emails everyone who subscribed, and it also stays on your Substack page forever. Readers can find it days, months, or years later.

It sits somewhere between a blog and Instagram. People subscribe with their email. You don't fight an algorithm. Your words and your photos go straight to the inbox of people who asked to hear from you.

For a photographer like you

Instagram rewards the scroll. A beautiful photo, a punchy caption, and the algorithm decides whether anyone sees it. Substack rewards something different: the story behind the photo. The waiting, the weather, the moment before the shot. The part of your work that doesn't fit in a caption.

Your superpower (images that make people want to pick up a camera themselves) translates beautifully here, because Substack readers aren't scrolling. They're reading. They want to know what you saw, how you saw it, and what you were doing there.

The honest picture
What's good about it
  • Readers actually read. Subscribers are higher-intent than followers.
  • No algorithm between you and your audience.
  • Your posts live forever, searchable and linkable.
  • You own the email list. If Substack disappeared tomorrow, you could take it with you.
  • Photographers who write well do unusually well here.
What's hard about it
  • More writing than Instagram. If you hate writing, this is a big ask.
  • Growth is slower. Subscribers come one at a time.
  • The platform is noisy with new writers. Standing out takes real voice.
  • Visuals look good but Substack is not built for big galleries. One or two strong images per post.
One last thing
You can turn on paid subscribers later, once you have readers who love your work. Don't worry about monetizing now. Just get the writing habit started.

Step 02 of 04 · Find your content

You already have most of what you need.

You don't have to write from scratch. You've been posting, captioning, and telling these stories for years on Instagram and Facebook. We're going to pull from that. Open your phone in another tab or window. You'll be copying and pasting a few things.

Pick a single photo you want this first post to be about. One image. The strongest one you've taken recently, or the one you're still thinking about weeks later. Have it open somewhere you can find it when you're ready to upload to Substack.

Plain description. A bald eagle mid-dive. A fox in morning light. Two black bears in a river. Whatever it is.

Check your Instagram or Facebook. Copy and paste anything you've written about this image or the scene it came from. It doesn't have to be polished. Even a few lines is plenty.

You don't have to write in full sentences yet. Just get it down. Where you were, how long you waited, what the weather was like, whether you almost gave up. The part of the story you don't usually share.

Why this matters
Every wildlife photographer has the photo. Almost none of them share the waiting, the cold, the false starts, the hour they almost left. That's the part your readers will fall in love with.

Step 03 of 04 · Put it together

Now we shape it into a post.

A good first post for a photographer follows a simple shape: a hook, the photo, the story, and a small invitation for the reader. We'll fill in each piece using what you already have. Don't overthink any of these. You can edit in Substack later.

Short. Specific. Curious. Not clever. "The Fox I Almost Didn't Wait For" beats "Red Fox in Winter" every time. What happened to you that day? Name it.

One line that tells the reader what they're about to read. It can be a small tease, a reflection, or just a plain description.

Drop the reader straight into the moment, or straight into a feeling. Not "I am a wildlife photographer and today I..." Instead: "I had been crouched in the same spot for two hours when the fox finally stepped out of the treeline." Pull from your backstory above.

This is the heart of the post. What happened before, during, and after the shot. What you were watching for. What almost went wrong. What you thought about while you waited. Take what you wrote in the backstory and clean it up into a few short paragraphs. Keep it honest, not polished.

One or two sentences. What this moment taught you, or what you keep thinking about, or what you hope other photographers take from it. Nothing grand. Just honest.

One line at the very end. A question, a note, or just "thanks for reading." This turns the post from a monologue into the start of a conversation. Optional but helps.

Where the photo goes
You'll upload the photo inside Substack itself, between the opening and the story. Don't worry about that here. Just write. The image slots in when you paste this into Substack.

Step 04 of 04 · Your draft

Here's your post, ready to go.

This is yours now. Copy it, paste it into Substack, upload your photo, and hit publish. You can edit anything at any time in Substack itself. What matters is that you have a real first post, made from real work you've already done.

How to actually post it

Five steps from draft to published.

  1. Go to substack.com and sign up if you haven't. Pick a publication name (you can change it later).
  2. Click New post in the top right.
  3. Paste your title in the title field, your subtitle in the subtitle field, and the rest of the body below.
  4. Click where you want the photo to go (after the opening is a safe bet), use the image icon, and upload your shot.
  5. Hit Publish. You can always edit it after. Send it to yourself first if you want to see how it looks in an inbox.
