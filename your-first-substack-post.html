Your First Substack Post

A guided walk-through

Welcome. Let's build your first Substack post.

By the end of this, you'll have an actual draft. Not a plan to write one. A real first post, ready to paste into Substack and hit publish. It should take about fifteen minutes. Go slow. Your work saves as you go, so you can close the tab and come back later if you need to.

Before we start
This is not a lecture. It's a workbook. I'll explain things as we go, and I'll ask you to paste your own work into a few boxes. Think of this as a first draft space, not a final anything. Perfect is the enemy here.

Step 01 of 04 · What it is

What Substack actually is, and whether it's for you.

Substack is an email newsletter that also lives on the web as your own little site. When you publish a post, it emails everyone who subscribed, and it also stays on your Substack page forever. Readers can find it days, months, or years later.

It sits somewhere between a blog and social media. People subscribe with their email. You don't fight an algorithm. Your words go straight to the inbox of people who asked to hear from you.

For someone with content already out there

Social platforms reward the scroll. A punchy caption, a clever thread, a clip — and the algorithm decides whether anyone sees it. Substack rewards something different: the longer thought behind the post. The story you couldn't fit in a caption. The argument that needed a thousand words instead of two hundred.

Your archive is an asset. Every old post, every saved draft, every tweet thread you wish more people had seen — that's raw material. We'll spend the next few minutes turning some of it into a real first issue.

For someone starting fresh

Starting from zero is harder in one way and easier in another. Harder because there's no archive to lean on. Easier because you don't have to negotiate with anyone's expectations of you. You get to decide what this is.

The trick is to use a repeatable shape for your first post — a small frame you can fill in this week, then fill in again next week, and again the week after. That's how a publication gets built. Not from one perfect post, but from a frame you can come back to.

The honest picture
What's good about it
  • Readers actually read. Subscribers are higher-intent than followers.
  • No algorithm between you and your audience.
  • Your posts live forever, searchable and linkable.
  • You own the email list. If Substack disappeared tomorrow, you could take it with you.
  • Long-form thinking is rewarded here in a way it isn't on most platforms.
What's hard about it
  • More writing than social. If you hate writing, this is a big ask.
  • Growth is slower. Subscribers come one at a time.
  • The platform is noisy with new writers. Standing out takes real voice.
  • It rewards consistency over brilliance. One great post matters less than ten honest ones.
One last thing
You can turn on paid subscribers later, once you have readers who love your work. Don't worry about monetizing now. Just get the writing habit started.

Step 02 of 04 · Find your content

You already have most of what you need.

You don't have to write from scratch. You've been posting, captioning, recording, or filming for a while now. We're going to pull from that. Open whichever platform holds your archive in another tab. You'll be copying and pasting a few things.

Step 02 of 04 · Find your seed

Start with one specific thing.

First posts go sideways when they try to introduce everything you'll ever write about. They go well when they pick one specific moment, lesson, or observation and go deep on it. We're going to find that one specific thing now.

Pick one piece of existing content you want this first post to grow out of. A post that got more replies than usual. A caption you're still proud of. A thread you wish more people had seen. A photo, a clip, a podcast episode, a section of an old essay. One thing.

A plain description in one sentence. e.g. A LinkedIn post about why my first product launch flopped. A photo of a fox at dawn. A podcast episode on parenting through burnout.

The actual caption, post, transcript, or excerpt. It doesn't have to be polished — that's the point. We're going to expand on it, not retype it. e.g. an Instagram caption, a tweet thread, a few paragraphs of a blog post, a podcast episode summary.

This is where the real Substack post lives. The story you cut for length. The detail you didn't think anyone would care about. The part that wouldn't fit in a caption or a thread. e.g. how long the project actually took. The conversation that changed your mind. The day you almost quit. The part you usually leave out.

Why this matters
Most creators have the headline version of their story already public. Almost none of them share the messy middle. That's the part your readers will fall in love with. That's also the part that turns a follower into a subscriber.

Pick one specific thing to write about. Not your whole topic. Not your whole story. One small, specific thing inside the bigger thing. The smaller you make it, the easier it is to write — and the more interesting it usually turns out to be.

Be specific. Not "leadership" — "the meeting where I realized I was the bottleneck." Not "running" — "what I think about during the last mile." Not "design" — "why I changed my mind about button labels." Specificity is your friend here.

Every good first post has a scene — a specific time, place, or event the rest of the post grows out of. Describe it. Where you were, who else was there, what was happening. Don't write it well yet. Just get it down. e.g. the morning your inbox broke you. The client call that changed your pricing. The first time a stranger quoted you back to yourself.

Why are you writing about this and not something else? What did this moment teach you, change about you, or make you see differently? Don't worry about being profound. Just be honest. e.g. it changed how I run meetings. It made me realize I'd been pricing out of fear. It taught me my work mattered to someone I'd never meet.

Why this matters
A first post doesn't need to be your manifesto. It needs to be one specific moment, told well, with one honest reflection. That's a repeatable shape. Once you've written one, you can write fifty.

Step 03 of 04 · Put it together

Now we shape it into a post.

A good first post follows a simple shape: a hook, the scene, the story or argument, a small reflection, and a quiet invitation for the reader. We'll fill in each piece using what you already have. Don't overthink any of these. You can edit in Substack later.

Short. Specific. Curious. Not clever. e.g. "The Fox I Almost Didn't Wait For" beats "Red Fox in Winter." "The Email That Changed My Pricing" beats "On Pricing." "What I Got Wrong About My Launch" beats "Lessons Learned." What actually happened to you? Name it.

One line that tells the reader what they're about to read. A small tease, a reflection, or a plain description. e.g. "Some shots don't happen until you've almost given up." "The pricing change I should have made two years earlier." "Three things I learned the hard way about launches."

Drop the reader straight into the moment, or straight into a feeling. Don't introduce yourself yet. Don't explain what the post is about. Start inside the scene. e.g. "I had been in the same spot for two hours when the fox finally stepped out." "The email arrived on a Tuesday and I read it four times before I understood what it said." Pull from what you wrote on the last screen.

This is the heart of the post. What happened before, during, and after. What you were thinking. What almost went wrong. What you noticed. Take what you wrote on the last screen and clean it up into a few short paragraphs. Keep it honest, not polished. Short paragraphs read better than long ones in an email.

One or two sentences. What this moment taught you, or what you keep thinking about, or what you hope readers take from it. Nothing grand. Just honest. e.g. "I think about that fox every time I want to leave a shoot early." "I still send that email to anyone considering raising their rates."

One line at the very end. A question, a note, or just "thanks for reading." This turns the post from a monologue into the start of a conversation. Optional but helps. e.g. "What's a moment you keep going back to? I'd love to hear about it." "Reply and tell me what you'd have done differently."

If you have a visual
If your post calls for an image — a photo, a chart, a screenshot — you'll add it inside Substack itself, between the opening and the story. Don't worry about that here. Just write. The image slots in when you paste this into Substack.

How to actually post it

Five steps from draft to published.

  1. Go to substack.com and sign up if you haven't. Pick a publication name (you can change it later).
  2. Click New post in the top right.
  3. Paste your title in the title field, your subtitle in the subtitle field, and the rest of the body below.
  4. If you want a visual, click where you want it to go (after the opening is a safe bet), use the image icon, and upload it.
  5. Hit Publish. You can always edit it after. Send it to yourself first if you want to see how it looks in an inbox.
