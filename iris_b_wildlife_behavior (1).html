Three signals every wildlife photographer reads.

Before you take a single step toward an animal, you are already reading it. These three body language signals tell you what the animal is feeling — and what your next move should be.

🦌

Safe to approach slowly

Relaxed and grazing

Head down, ears relaxed and moving independently, tail hanging loose. The animal is focused on feeding and has not registered your presence.

Move slowly, stay low, use available cover. This is your window.

👁️

Stop. Wait. Do not advance.

Alert and watching

Head up, ears locked forward, eyes fixed on you. The animal knows you are there. It is deciding whether you are a threat. Any movement now will tip the decision.

Freeze completely. Let it return to grazing before you move again.

⚠️

Back off. Shot is gone.

Flagging and stomping

Tail raised high (the white "flag"), front foot stomping the ground, body turned sideways. The animal is alarmed and signaling to others. Flight is seconds away.

Back away slowly. Chasing a fleeing animal damages the habitat and ends your session.

Three field situations. What do you do?

You are in the field with your camera. Read what the animal is doing and decide your next move.

Early morning meadow — you are 60 yards away

Situation 01 of 03

A doe is feeding 60 yards ahead. Head down, ears loose, tail relaxed. She has not looked up in several minutes.

The light is perfect. You are downwind. There is a line of shrubs between you and her that could get you to 30 yards.

Edge of a forest clearing — she is staring directly at you

Situation 02 of 03

You step around a tree and a doe locks eyes with you. Head up, ears pinned forward, body completely still. She is staring directly at you.

You are 35 yards away. Your camera is raised. The shot would be incredible — but she has not moved yet.

Woodland trail — white tail raised, front foot has stomped twice
Woodland trail — white tail raised, front foot has stomped twice

Situation 03 of 03

A buck has his tail raised high — you can see the white flag clearly. He has stomped his front foot twice and is turning his body sideways.

You never got a shot. He spotted you before you saw him. You are 25 yards away on a narrow trail.
